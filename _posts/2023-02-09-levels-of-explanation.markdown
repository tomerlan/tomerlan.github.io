The debate over whether an ailment is physical or psychological often arises, with some asserting that the distinction is meaningless due to the interplay between the two. Chronic pain, for instance, is said to have either psychological or organic causes. Yet since the brain, where psychology occurs, is itself physical, some conclude that the divide between physical and psychological is futile altogether.

However, this distinction is not meaningless. It is similar to the distinction between various branches of science, such as physics and chemistry, or chemistry and biology. While everything can be ultimately reduced to physics, some phenomena are better explained by different levels of analysis. For example, the stock market can in principle be explained (in the sense of predicted) by understanding individual atoms, just like behavior can be fully predicted by understanding the action of neurons. But this is completely impractical. To understand these phenomena you need to study the emergent properties with their own rules and explanations.

When determining if a medical issue is physical or psychological, it's important to ask which level of explanation will be most effective in resolving it. If the problem can be resolved through talk therapy, it's considered psychological. If medication is the only solution, it's considered chemical. If both methods are effective, then it's considered both. As our understanding of the world advances, the distinction between physical and psychological may blur, but the level of explanation will always be tied to our current understanding and ability to perceive the problem at hand.
