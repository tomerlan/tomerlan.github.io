Teleportation is a theoretical concept in physics and science fiction where an object or a person is instantaneously transferred from one point in space to another, without traveling through the intervening space. Currently, the closest we have come to teleportation is by exploiting a quantum mechanical phenomenon called entanglement, which allows us to copy a quantum state to a distant location (caveat: there are interpretational nuances here that I am neglecting). This process involves sending a signal to the remote location, which is limited by the speed of light, and therefore it is not instantaneous. More importantly, it is not truly moving an object from one place to another, but rather destroying it in one location and recreating an identical object elsewhere (different particles but same structure). However, since any two particles are fundamentally indistinguishable, this is inconsequential. Or is it?

Suppose we could actually teleport an entire person, but that would entail destroying the person in one location and recreating an identical copy in another location. Would you be willing to get teleported?

Amusingly, I feel that my own answer depends on factors it should not depend on. The atoms in our bodies are in constant turnover, so I don’t feel my identity is tied to the particular ones that comprise me at a given moment. In that respect I don’t mind going through with this. It’s the timing that matters to me. If the procedure could be performed instantaneously, then I find it easiest to digest. If on the other hand there is a lag, I would be a bit uncomfortable. It means that there is an instance where I do not exist. Even if I neglect the possibility of something interfering with the process at that instance, it’s still unsettling. Worst though is if there is a reverse lag, i.e. that I am destroyed only after the replica has been created. Imagine you could even see your replica at a distance. Now, would you stay and die or would you run?

If you chose to run, it couldn’t be because you are differentiated from that replica by a few seconds of memory. We forget things all the time and we don’t perceive this as dying. But once the two of you are around simultaneously, it feels as if there are two separate beings to consider.

Our current understanding of physics renders all three scenarios fundamentally impractical. So while this is a purely hypothetical question, it highlights an absurdity in our self-perception. The relative timing of the destruction and creation events affects my willingness to undergo teleportation, even though it has no impact on the outcome. However, if faced with a reality where teleportation is a common occurrence, it's likely that one would adjust and become accustomed to the concept, regardless of the specific scenario.
