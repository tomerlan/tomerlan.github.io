The question posed this way may be perplexing at first, as if it is seeking an explanation for something too basic to require one. Upon further inspection, a common response is that we remember the past because it already happened and we don’t remember the future because it has yet to happen. In other words, the asymmetry in our memory reflects an ontological asymmetry - the past exists and the future doesn’t. Some notable philosophers have also posited that the answer to this question is rooted in the metaphysics of time.

Of course, this is nonsense. The asymmetry of memory is an empirically verifiable phenomenon that must have a scientific explanation. It cannot be “explained” by employing metaphysical mumbo jumbo. In the scientific picture of the world as described by physics, the past and future are not ontologically different. In a deterministic world, they are equally determined, and in a non-deterministic world, they are equally undetermined. So where should we look for the answer?

The encoding of memories occurs in the brain, therefore, the difference in memory between the past and future may be related to how the brain processes information. However, it is unlikely that a creature with a different brain architecture would have the ability to remember the future and not the past or to remember both. Similarly, advancements in technology are unlikely to grant us the ability to remember the future. Thus, the explanation for this phenomenon must go beyond the realm of brain function and biology and into something more fundamental.

The solution to this mystery lies in the realm of physics, specifically the physics of macroscopic systems composed of multiple particles. A memory can be compared to a footprint in the sand or a photograph. The reason we only have records of past events and not future ones, such as footprints of past travelers or photographs of people from the past, is due to the arrow of time dictated by thermodynamics for macroscopic phenomena.

The Second Law of Thermodynamics states that entropy tends to increase over time. If we were to watch a video of a footprint being made in reverse, we would see a process in which entropy decreases, which goes against the Second Law (statistically). It is similar to a video of a sugar cube dissolving in water watched in reverse. This is actually what dictates the direction of causality. Therefore, memories, as a macroscopic phenomenon, can only be caused by past events, and hence they only record the past. The direction of the past is determined by the point in time when the universe had low entropy.
