Dinosaurs were the largest animals to ever roam the earth. Our knowledge of them, including their size, is based on the fossil records that have been discovered. However, it's important to note that our fossil records represent a tiny fraction of all the dinosaurs that have lived during their 165 million year reign. This means that some of the largest species of dinosaurs are only known through a few specimens.

Animal size can vary greatly, just like how Shaquille O'Neal is more than twice my size in terms of lean mass. Of course, this is the far end of the bell curve. Given this variability and its distribution, it is statistically unlikely that we have found fossils belonging to the largest individuals within a species. With limited fossils available for study, it's fair to assume they belong to average-sized specimens. This sample is especially skewed if dinosaurs grew continuously throughout life like reptiles. Among reptiles, only a very small fraction of the population represents the maximum size for a given species, so a small sample of fossils is unlikely to reflect the full size potential of the species.

The largest dinosaur currently known is the Argentinosaurus, with an estimated weight of up to 100 tons. Based on the rationale presented, it is possible that much larger dinosaurs existed, of that species or perhaps of undiscovered species, possibly weighing 200 tons. This uncertainty is even greater with marine animals, who’s fossils are even more scarce. It also applies to the biggest carnivores like T-Rex. Food for thought, @Steven Spielberg.
