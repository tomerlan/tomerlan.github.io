Many believe that health is the most important aspect of life, but at the same time, they resist efforts to tackle aging, which is the primary threat to health. Others may spend top dollar fighting the aesthetic outcomes of aging, but still consider the idea of fighting aging itself highly objectionable. The prevalence of this discrepancy in the public can be frustrating for those who are dedicated to combating aging. This dissonance is so widespread due to the widely held belief that aging is inevitable and there is little that can be done about it. But contrary to this belief, scientific evidence suggests that aging may be controllable and potentially even prevented or reversed, leading to the possibility of a lifespan beyond the natural limit of 120 years, known as “radical longevity”.

Due to the widespread misconception that aging is unavoidable, people have adopted either an "aging is good" mentality as a means of coping with their imminent decrepitude and death, or engage in denial by believing in an afterlife, or simply avoid the thought altogether. These mindsets make even health-conscious individuals who otherwise support medical advancement, passionately oppose any efforts to alter the aging process. It is hard to get them to reconsider their position because it entails suspending their coping mechanisms and confronting the blight of aging, risking disappointment if it is eventually proven to be unavoidable.

These coping strategies can actually hinder progress by perpetuating the view that aging is an accepted part of life. For instance, aging is not recognized as a disease by regulatory agencies, leading to limited public funding for aging research. The belief that aging is inevitable creates a self-reinforcing cycle: believe it's inevitable → develop coping mechanisms → don't question the belief → believe it's inevitable.

Other common reasons for objecting to radical longevity:
* Misunderstanding: People may believe that extending lifespan means being old and frail for a longer time, but proponents of radical longevity actually aim to extend healthy years of life. Clarifying this often resolves objections.
* Fear of the Unknown: Radical ideas often evoke strong negative responses due to the potential for unforeseeable negative consequences, along with obvious benefits. People are naturally cautious of such unknowns.
* Fear of Technology: Some fear technology due to its potentially destructive power, which is intensified by dystopian portrayals in books and films. Despite its past negative uses, the benefits of technology likely outweigh the cost.
* Conservatism: Religious beliefs can clash with the concept of radical longevity, as some may consider death as a natural aspect of life and view efforts to change it as blasphemy. This represents a culturally ingrained coping mechanism.
* Depression: Some have shitty lives and understandably they do not wish to prolong them.
* Disruption: Individuals who are content with their current life may not wish to disrupt their legacy or established plans. It's similar to a prisoner who is unhappy with a pardon after they have adjusted to life in prison.

People may seek to rationalize their objections, citing concerns such as overpopulation or depletion of retirement funds, but the true reasons for objecting are likely one or more of the above. By highlighting the underlying reasons, the absurdity of objections to radical longevity can be appreciated.
