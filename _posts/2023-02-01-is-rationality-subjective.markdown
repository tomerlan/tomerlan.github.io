The world is full of contentious subjects, where individuals often have conflicting views on the truth due to differing dispositions, state of knowledge, and personal judgment. While it is not possible for two people to disagree and both be right at the same time, as there is always a fact of the matter, it might be possible for two individuals to examine the same evidence, operate under the same assumptions and still disagree while both being rational. This can happen if they carried different procedures for obtaining evidence. This variability in the process of obtaining evidence helps explain the widespread disagreement that exists on numerous subjects.

For illustration, consider a hypothesis that I have psychic abilities. To verify it, I try to predict lottery numbers. If I succeed, I will conclude that I am either psychic or just made a lucky guess. I would likely lean towards the former explanation, and this would be a rational choice from my perspective. However, if I heard this story secondhand, I would be more inclined to attribute it to pure chance. This doesn't stem from a lack of credibility in the story, but rather from the numerous instances in history where coincidences have occurred. The specific experiment may have had a one in a billion chance of succeeding, but it is much more likely for something of this sort to happen somewhere at some point in time, and that I hear about it due to its uniqueness. From a secondhand perspective, this is not an isolated experiment to test the psychic hypothesis but one out of many opportunities for coincidences to occur.

Thus, despite having the same information in both scenarios - either by conducting the experiment myself or hearing about it secondhand - my level of confidence in the evidence varies, leading to varying conclusions. 
 
In more technical language, the same data can result in differing levels of evidence based on the different sets of hypotheses being considered. If one individual tests a specific hypothesis on a given dataset, while another tests the same hypothesis along with another million, the former may have a higher confidence in their hypothesis, even though both tested it. The individual testing a million hypotheses would perform a correction for multiple hypotheses testing and has a higher likelihood of considering this particular case to be a false positive.
