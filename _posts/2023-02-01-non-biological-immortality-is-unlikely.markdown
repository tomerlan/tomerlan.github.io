In my view, aging is the most critical issue facing humanity due to its adverse impact on health and lifespan restrictions. Current attempts to counteract aging are focused on the biological aspect, by studying and modifying the biological causes. However, alternative methods that do not rely on biology can potentially offer a quicker route to immortality.

The concept of "mind upload" is often discussed in transhumanist circles. It's based on the idea that our identity is not rooted in biology, but rather in the way our mind functions as a program running on the brain as hardware. Our personality and experience are specifics of this program. If this program could be transferred to a durable, non-biological medium like a computer chip, it's believed that one could effectively avoid aging and other biological limitations, while maintaining his identity. Potentially, we could also experience additional benefits like enhanced capabilities through software updates.

While it sounds very appealing in a futuristic sort of way, I find this idea to be both impractical and philosophically flawed. Firstly, I struggle to see how this can be achieved. Perhaps it's my limited knowledge or imagination. But, how can the state of the brain be measured with such precision and replicated accurately? Currently, our tools for brain measurement are rudimentary, making it hard to imagine this becoming a reality in the near future. To me, finding a biological cure for aging seems more achievable. I cannot provide a strong argument to support this, as it's difficult to prove something is impossible. However, from my understanding, this is not a practical solution in the foreseeable future. Additionally, any such technology will be destructive to the brain, by slicing it up or whatnot, to perform the necessary measurements (assuming brain states are classical and not quantum states that can be measured without being irrevocably affected). This raises a concern about identity, because you are actually creating a replica of yourself which is a separate entity.

Indeed, even if we successfully create a digital copy of our minds, it will only be a replica, while the original body is either destroyed or remains subject to aging. Would you be willing to sacrifice your physical self if a perfect replica of yourself existed or guaranteed to be created?

Yet another point to consider is the fidelity of the replica. How would you exhaustively verify it? And how would you tell if it is conscious? Passing the Turing test may not imply consciousness but mere successful imitation. Consider the Chinese room thought experiment. Here is how chatGPT explains it:

	“The Chinese Room thought experiment is a philosophical thought experiment proposed by John Searle in 1980. It aims to challenge the idea that a computer program can truly understand language and have genuine intelligence, as opposed to merely simulating it.
	The experiment is designed to illustrate the limitations of the Turing Test, which is a test of a machine's ability to exhibit intelligent behavior equivalent to, or indistinguishable from, that of a human. The experiment goes as follows:

	Imagine a person who does not speak or understand Chinese, locked inside a room. The person is given a set of rules, in English, for manipulating Chinese symbols. People outside the room then pass in slips of paper with Chinese characters written on them. The person inside the room uses the rules to manipulate the Chinese characters, and then passes back out slips of paper with Chinese characters written on them.

	From the outside, it appears as though the person inside the room understands Chinese, as they are able to receive and respond to Chinese characters. However, the person inside the room does not actually understand Chinese, they are simply following a set of rules in order to manipulate symbols.

	The point of the experiment is to argue that this scenario is analogous to how computers manipulate symbols based on algorithms, without truly understanding the meaning behind the symbols. In other words, the Chinese Room thought experiment suggests that the ability to manipulate symbols and perform tasks does not necessarily equate to true understanding or intelligence, and that there may be something more to human cognition that cannot be replicated by computers.“

The last point is more nuanced. Mind upload is premised on the notion that mental states, and ultimately the mind, are reducible to patterns that are independent of their specific physical manifestation (in this case neuronal activity). The hypothesis that the mind is an abstract algorithm implemented in the brain implies that it can be described as a state machine. But in principle, every simple physical system, owing to the vastness of the microscopic physical state space and the non-circular dynamics, can be interpreted as implementing _any._ state machine. Therefore, one can argue that a glass of water is implementing e.g. Windows by _ad hoc._ matching its internal states to those of a computer running Windows. And by the same token, one can also argue that it implements your mind, leading to the absurd conclusion that your mind is implemented infinite times by everything in the Universe. To me, this suggests that the specific physical medium, the brain in this case, is an integral part of the mind and consciousness.

Given its technological impracticality, and because it is such a philosophical mindfield, I am not placing any hope on “mind upload” to solve aging and death. The only non-biological idea I would consider is gradual brain replacement, i.e., slowly adding artificial components such that they integrate into the biological brain.
