In my view, aging is the most critical issue facing humanity due to its adverse impact on health and lifespan restrictions. Current attempts to counteract aging are focused on the biological aspect, by studying and modifying the biological causes. However, alternative methods that do not rely on biology can potentially offer a quicker route to immortality.

The concept of "mind upload" is often discussed in transhumanist circles. It's based on the idea that our identity is not rooted in biology, but rather in the way our mind functions as a program running on the brain as hardware. Our personality and experience are specifics of this program. If this program could be transferred to a durable, non-biological medium like a computer chip, it's believed that one could effectively avoid aging and other biological limitations, while maintaining his identity. Potentially, we could also experience additional benefits like enhanced capabilities through software updates.

While it sounds very appealing in a futuristic sort of way, I find this idea to be both impractical and philosophically flawed. Firstly, I struggle to see how this can be achieved. Perhaps it's my limited knowledge or imagination. But, how can the state of the brain be measured with such precision and replicated accurately? Currently, our tools for brain measurement are rudimentary, making it hard to imagine this becoming a reality in the near future. To me, finding a biological cure for aging seems more achievable. I cannot provide a strong argument to support this, as it's difficult to prove something is impossible. However, from my understanding, this is not a practical solution at present. Additionally, any such technology will be destructive to the brain, by slicing it up or whatnot, which brings us back to the identity issue. And this is assuming brain states are classical (and not quantum) states that can be measured without being irrevocably affected.

Secondly, there is a question of identity. If we successfully create a digital copy of our minds, it will only be a duplicate, while the original body remains subject to aging. Would you be willing to sacrifice your physical self if a perfect replica of yourself existed? And what would convince you that you are indeed replicated? If an entity responds in a manner that cannot be differentiated from you, does it necessarily imply consciousness?

The last point is more subtle. Mind upload is premised on the notion that mental states, and ultimately the mind, are reducible to patterns that are independent of their specific physical manifestation (in this case neuronal activity). This is problematic because in essence every simple physical system, owing to the vastness of the microscopic physical state space and the non-circular dynamics, can be interpreted as implementing any pattern/software. So in principle we can argue that a glass of water is implementing e.g. Windows by ad hoc matching its internal states to those of a computer running Windows. You can do the same thing to a human mind if you are able to describe it as a software. This leads me to believe that the physical medium is an integral part of the mind. I recognize that this point might sound somewhat opaque without further explanation. For a more detailed discussion, search for “multiple implantation problem”, which is a topic in philosophy of mind.

Given its technological impracticality, and because it is such a philosophical mindfield, I am not placing any hope on “mind upload” to solve aging and death. The only non-biological idea I would consider is gradual brain replacement, i.e., slowly adding artificial components such that they integrate into the biological brain.
